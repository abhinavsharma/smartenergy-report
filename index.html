About the Project

The Smart Energy Visualization project is a collaborative project between Bosch Research and the Carnegie Mellon Undergraduate Human-Computer Interaction department. Through our research, we will discover what information is most relevant to users’ energy consumption. From there, we will seek to create an actionable interface that increases energy awareness for users and builds our skill sets in user research, data visualization, and overall interface usability. We will determine a target population of interest and create an information display meant to influence the users’ behavior related to energy conservation.

The goal of the project is to make use of Bosch’s non-intrusive load monitoring sensors to provide bill-payers with actionable information on their energy usage. Our preliminary audience included home owners, landlords and facility managers; this audience has been narrowed based on the results of our user-research. Through our research, we gathered information to evaluate the incentives users desire through interviews and discovered what information is most actionable, leading to a refined specificity of the information they require.

Key Takeaways

Through our user research process documented below, we narrowed the scope of the project to home owners. Furthermore, specific to home owners, we listed some design goals:

  • Disaggregated information has been proven to be effective in helping save energy (from literature review)
  • Goal setting helps with curtailing energy usage if the goals are specific.
  • There is a very wide range of how much home owners care about energy use.
  • Home owners do not consider their electric bills to be private or sensitive information
  • Users would like to be made aware of anomalies in their energy usage.

User Research Report

Platforms

In the early part of the design process, we considered different interfaces for the system to potentially use. These were:

  • Mobile: The major advantage of using a mobile interface is that its easy for the user to access from wherever they are. The major disadvantage is that the screen is small and thus may be inconvenient for complex visualizations. Through iteration, we realized that people want simple visualizations and thus eliminated the need for a very large screen. We thus chose mobile for our final implementation.
  • Web: A web based display is good because it is accessible from a large number of devices but is still large enough to accommodate complex visualizations and graphs. However, it is not as accessible as mobile.
  • Wall Mounted: The main advantage to a wall-mounted display is that it is dedicated to the device and does not have the overhead of requiring a computer/phone to operate. However, it does not have the mobility of a phone and adds overhead cost.

We storyboarded and sketched frames for each use case to help us with walking through potential use cases with users while speed-dating the concepts

Personas

In order to accommodate the needs of our different user groups, we generated three very different personas. As we worked through concepts, we related concepts to the mindset of each of these personas to see if our solution would be consistent with the needs of each kind of user.

Design Implications from Research

Ambient Information

We learnt from research that users do not want to constantly keep checking on their energy usage. Instead, they would prefer being notified of any anomalies or significant changes in their usage behavior.

We have made this a core feature of our application by making the home page have a notification system that adapts over time to the user's needs.

  • Actions Inbox on home page
  • Suggested actions
  • Previously taken actions.
  • Previously taken actions.

Diverse User Needs

We discovered through our user research that even within home owners the information requirement of the audience was greatly varied. The one common need was to be told of anomalies through a messaging system.

Thus, we designed the home page to be flexible and work with interchageble widgets that the user can enable and disable to get the information most relevant to them on their home page.

  • Energy Budget Widget.
  • Live Usage Widget.

Willingness to Share

We discovered that people do not consider their energy usage information to be private or sensitive and are thus willing to share it with others. To leverage this, we designed methods to help people compare their usage information with friends as well as a reference point for other similar households.

  • Choose friends to compare to
  • Comparison to friend and an intelligently determined average

Effectively Using Disaggregated Information

Our literature review proved that disaggregated information was effective in lowering energy use but did not mention exactly how it was used. We visualized disaggregated information in multiple ways including the ability to see usage information by device.

  • Appliance list sorted by usage
  • Time series for each appliance

Also, we allow users to compare energy usage between their devices using a treemap, which we chose over a pie chart because it scales better to more devices.

  • Appliance usage breakdown

Future Work

We tried incorporating most ideas possible into our design. However, since we were constantly iterating until the end, there are some design ideas as well as important points that we would like to point out as important when designing such a system in the future

  • Web Version: We initially prototyped a web version of the product that we then abandoned due to time constraints. However, we originally imagined this as a combination of a web and mobile interface. The larger screen is better suited to detailed analysis of the data and higher fidelity visualizations.
  • Other Audiences: A large part of our initial research was focused on determining use cases for this system for lanlords and facility managers. We ultimately chose to design for home owners because of ease of access and their control over the household, however we found strong demand for the system within small business owners and landlords. These audiences should by no means be considered a bad fit for the system.
  • Notifications and Feedback: The notification system was designed to allow for user feedback. It is critical that the system learns from feedback and adjusts to showing or hiding similar information in the future depending on the user's choices. The notification system itself would require significantly more behavioral study to determine effective techniques for changing user behavior over time. Our intention with the notification system was to put in place a framework for communicating directly about issues with the user. However, the content for this system is in itself a very challenging problem.
  • Extending Widgets: The development of home page widgets as a flexible interchangeble set of visualizations was a fairly late decision in the design process. Since the intent of widgets is to allow the user to have the information most useful to them on the home page, widgets could be extended with more visualizations as well as the ability to put up graphs for certain devices or any other page in the website as a widget.
